## 1. Core premise

A discovery call is not where you sell. It is where you:

1. Qualify whether the prospect is even worth working with.
2. Surface a problem painful and concrete enough that the prospect will pay to fix it.
3. Set yourself up for one of three outcomes (see §2).

A "successful" call is not defined by a signature. It is defined by whether you ran the process correctly. Coached agencies have moved from ~13% close rate to ~51% close rate using this framework. The remaining 49% are mostly disqualified prospects — which is a feature, not a failure.

Common failure mode: agencies obsess over booking the call and then under-invest in running it. Outcome is rushed pitches, premature pricing, and prospects who ghost.

---

## 2. The three valid outcomes

| Outcome | When | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **No-go** | Prospect lacks quantifiable problem, urgency, or budget signal | Politely walk. Saves the prospect's money and your time. |
| **Audit (recommended default)** | Custom-build engagement; scope cannot be priced in 45 minutes | A paid audit lets you scope properly. Maps directly to multi-week implementation businesses. |
| **One-call close** | Productized offer with known cost structure | Only valid for repeatable offers where pricing has been pre-calculated. |

The framework recommends the audit route for most AI agencies. Pricing custom AI builds inside a 45-minute window relies on too many assumptions and either underprices the work or scares the prospect.

## 3. Foundations

These are pre-conditions before stage 1. The framework explicitly calls these out as more important than any tactical script.

### 3.1 State (mental/emotional)

- The prospect feels your state. Doubt about the offer leaks through tone, pace, and word choice.
- Detach from the outcome. Murphy's-law type principle: the more you cling to closing, the more you fail at closing.
- Take a breath before the call. Remove the pressure consciously.

### 3.2 Presentation

- Camera ON, mic decent (audio matters more than video quality).
- Avoid suit-and-tie formality. Aim for "living-room" comfort. Pressured environments shut prospects down.
- Default expectation: the prospect's camera is also on. If off, ask politely: *"Hey, can you see me? I can't see you."* — 9 out of 10 turn it on.

### 3.3 Pace

- Talk slower than feels natural. Nervousness compresses speech.
- Slow pace signals authority and care. Fast pace signals selling pressure.

## 4. Stage 1 — Frame

**Purpose:** Establish authority by setting expectations, not by talking loudly. Get a micro-commitment to your rules so future redirects don't feel confrontational.
**Time:** 0–3 min.

### 4.1 The doctor analogy

The frame mimics a doctor's appointment: limited time, structured questions, diagnosis-driven.

**Script template:**
```
"Thanks for hopping on. Typically how these calls go — I'll ask you a few
questions, kind of like going to the doctor. Based on your answers, I'll
diagnose if we can help. If you give vague answers, I won't be able to. If
you go too long, I'll pull you back. About 45 minutes, and at the end I'll
point you toward one of three things — either we're not the right fit, we
go deeper with an audit, or we look at how to start. Sound good?"
```

### 4.2 The micro-commitment

The closing *"Sound good?"* (or *"Sounds fair?"* / *"Klinkt dat goed?"*) is non-negotiable. It is the prospect's verbal opt-in to the rules of the call. Without it, every later redirect feels like an interruption rather than a follow-through on a stated agreement.

### 4.3 Time boxing

- 45 minutes total. Brain saturation kicks in around that mark; information after 45 min stops being absorbed and retained.
- 50 minutes is acceptable. Beyond that, the prospect is mentally checked out.
- Set the time expectation at the start so redirects ("we have 45 minutes, let me get back to the question…") have a verbal anchor.

### 4.4 Three outcomes pre-announcement

State the three outcomes in the frame itself. This conditions the prospect to expect a possible no — which paradoxically makes them more open and less defensive.

## 5. Stage 2 — Discovery

**Purpose:** Surface, isolate, and quantify the prospect's problem. This is where 80% of value is created. Skipping or rushing it makes every later stage harder.
**Time:** 3–18 min (~15 minutes — the longest single stage).

### 5.1 Break the ice — anchor back to "yes"

```
"Out of curiosity, what made you actually jump on this call?"
```

**Why:** No one books discovery calls casually. Some prior signal (your outreach, an ad, a referral, a piece of content) made them act. Forcing them to vocalize that signal pulls them back into the buying state they were in when they booked. Once they say it out loud, it is "on the board" — you can reference it for the rest of the call.

**Read the response type:**
- **Quick/short answer:** early resistance. Trust not yet earned.
- **Story / detailed answer:** they're already opening up. Capture details.

### 5.2 Handle early resistance

Common early-resistance signals: *"just tell me the price"*, *"what do you do exactly?"*, immediate diving into their solution preferences before you've asked anything.

**Tactic:** Never refuse directly. Use a third-party analogy that puts common sense between you and them.

**Car / garage analogy (for price questions):**
```
"Totally fair question. Quick one — when your car breaks down, do you tell
the garage the price, or do they run a diagnosis first and then quote you?
Same thing here. I genuinely don't know what to charge until I understand
the problem. Mind if I ask a few questions first?"
```

**Doctor analogy (for "just tell me what to do"):**
```
"Look, if a doctor just handed you a prescription as you walked in, would
you trust they'd done due diligence? Same thing — I need to ask first."
```

### 5.3 Handle storytellers

Prospect goes on a 5-minute monologue that is not relevant to the diagnosis. Polite interruption pattern:

```
"[Name] — really appreciate the detail. Quick one though..."
[next question]
```

Because of the frame's micro-commitment, the prospect cannot reasonably take offense. If they do, that itself is signal of poor fit.

### 5.4 Isolate ONE specific problem

```
"What specifically do you need help with?"
```

If they list multiple things:
```
"Sounds like there's a few moving parts. What's the one that needs fixing
today?"
```

A list is unsellable. One problem with weight is sellable.

### 5.5 Layer the problem

```
"What makes you feel like you need help with that?"
"When did this first come up in a meeting?"
```

The "in a meeting" phrasing is deliberate and powerful:
- Anchors the prospect's brain to a concrete recall point (better than vague time).
- Qualifies budget reality: if a problem has never been raised in a meeting, it will not get budget.
- Pre-empts the "we're just exploring" cop-out.

### 5.6 Quantify — turn pain into a number

This is the single most leverage move in the framework. B2B buyers act on logic and numbers, not feelings.

**Method (worked example):**
- Prospect: *"We spend an hour a day on manual data entry."*
- You: *"Got it. Quick math — if the person doing it earns £50k, that's about £26/hour. An hour a day is roughly £600 a month, or £7,200 a year. Does that line up with what you'd estimate?"*

The problem stops being "an hour a day" and becomes £7,200/year. That number is what justifies the audit fee and the eventual build.

**Quantifier menu by problem type:**

| Problem | Quantifier questions |
|---|---|
| Manual time waste | "How many people, how many hours, what's their cost/hour?" |
| Lost leads | "How many inquiries/week? What % do you lose? Average deal value? Close rate?" |
| Slow response | "How many deals do you reckon you lose to slow responses?" |
| Customer churn | "Average customer lifetime value? How many leaving per month?" |
| Quote/proposal lag | "How many proposals slip past their window? Average value × close rate?" |
| Data hygiene | "How long does someone spend hunting for data? × number of hunts/week?" |

### 5.7 Stay in the pain (Hormozi principle)

Once the problem is named and quantified, do not rush off it. Ask additional layers — impact on team, on growth, on the founder's energy. The longer the prospect stays inside the problem, the more motivated they are to spend money to leave it.

**Time anchor for the stage:** ~15 minutes spent here is not too long. It is the right amount.

## 6. Stage 3 — Pre-handling

**Purpose:** Surface objections BEFORE the pitch, while there is still time to reframe.
**Time:** 18–22 min.

### 6.1 The pre-handling question

Tie it to the quantified problem to maximize urgency:

```
"Knowing this has been costing you ~£600/month for over a year, what's
gotten in the way of actually fixing it?"
```

### 6.2 The three response patterns

| Response | What it really means | Probe |
|---|---|---|
| **"We haven't done anything"** | Behavioral pattern; usually unspoken money or time issue | *"Out of curiosity — what stopped you from just trying ChatGPT or n8n yourself?"* |
| **"We tried [other agency]"** | Gold. They will list the criteria they want from you. | *"Help me understand — what was it about that company that didn't meet the expectations?"* |
| **"We've just started looking"** | Time-association mismatch | *"How long has the problem existed? What changed that put it on the agenda this week?"* |

### 6.3 The DIY-attempt assumption

If the prospect says they have done nothing: assume they have at least once attempted a DIY fix (ChatGPT prompt, Zapier flow, Make scenario, hire). Probe for it. The DIY-failure story is gold — it positions you as the expert who can do what they couldn't.

### 6.4 The previous-agency story

If they cite a previous agency, listen for both:
- **The objective failure** (what they want from you) — usually surfaces as criteria like "ongoing support", "responsiveness", "industry knowledge".
- **The bias** — they will tell the story in a way that makes them look like the victim. Filter for "client-from-hell" red flags.

Soft-phrased probe (less confrontational):
```
"Help me understand what was it about that client/agency that didn't meet
the expectations you were hoping for?"
```

### 6.5 The principle

The objection a prospect raises at minute 20 is the same objection they will raise at minute 45 when you ask for the signature. Surfacing it now means you can reframe it inside the pitch instead of fighting it after.

## 7. Stage 4 — Alignment

**Purpose:** Get the prospect to verbalize their buying criteria *before* you pitch, so you can mirror them back.
**Time:** 22–26 min.

### 7.1 Core question

```
"If I were sitting in your office tomorrow helping you fix this — what
would you be expecting from me? How would you want this to work?"
```

### 7.2 Caddy analogy (for prospects who want you to lead)

```
"Imagine I'm Tiger Woods and you're about to play a round of golf, and
I'm your caddy. You've got the best problem-solver in the corner — but
the caddy still needs to know what the player wants. Same here."
```

(This is intentionally absurd but it works to lower the prospect's guard and make them spell out their preferences.)

### 7.3 What you are listening for

| Signal | How they phrase it | What you do with it |
|---|---|---|
| Deadline | *"We need this by Q3"* | Becomes pitch pillar #2 or #3 |
| Budget envelope | *"We've got around £X allocated"* | Sizes the offer |
| Communication preference | *"Weekly check-ins"* / *"WhatsApp updates"* | Becomes pitch pillar |
| Industry expertise | *"Someone who knows our sector"* | Reframes audit as the path to that knowledge |
| Reliability | *"We need it to actually work"* | Becomes pitch pillar — links to ownership/maintenance |

### 7.4 What you are NOT doing

You are NOT pitching yet. Resist the urge to confirm "we do all of those things". Just collect.

---

## 8. Stage 5 — Vision

**Purpose:** Make the future state vivid, quantified, and specific. Create the gap.
**Time:** 26–32 min.

### 8.1 Core question

```
"Let's say tomorrow this is fixed. What does that actually unlock for the
business?"
```

### 8.2 The Larry pattern

The framework's anchor example. Apply universally:

- A team member ("Larry") spends 1 hour/day on a task that costs the company £600/month.
- Vision question: *"What would Larry do with that hour back?"*
- Prospect: *"Actually, Larry could finally do [task X] that we've been putting off for two years."*
- Quantify task X: *"What would task X be worth?"*
- Prospect: *"Probably £1,000/month."*
- **Now the gap is £1,600/month, not £600/month.**

The gap is what the prospect is paying you to bridge. Bigger gap = stronger motivation = easier pitch.

### 8.3 The selling principle

You are not selling time savings. You are selling transformation. The transformation is current state → future state, with you as the bridge.

### 8.4 Layered vision questions

```
"What would this do for the team?"
"What would [Larry] feel like coming in tomorrow if this were fixed?"
"What does this unlock for [growth area]?"
```

The longer the prospect spends inside the future, the more attached they get to it.

---

## 9. Stage 6 — Point of no return

**Purpose:** Last objection sweep before you start pitching.
**Time:** 32–34 min.

### 9.1 The sweep question

```
"Before I get into how we'd actually help — is there anything else I should
know?"
```

### 9.2 What you are pulling out

The objection that has been sitting at the back of the prospect's mind for the last 10 minutes. Almost always one of:
- Price anxiety
- Timing concern
- Decision-maker absence ("I'd need to run this past my partner")
- Specific feature/integration concern
- Trust signal request ("can I see case studies?")

### 9.3 Do not answer yet — probe

```
"Totally — I'll cover that in a moment. Quick one though, what specifically
about [cost/timing/X] is important for me to know?"
```

You are still collecting ammunition for the pitch.

---

## 10. Stage 7 — Pitch

**Purpose:** Convert into either audit sale, product sale, or clean no-go.
**Time:** 34–43 min.

### 10.1 The opening — restate their world in their language

```
"Right — thanks for being so open. So based on what you've told me: right
now you're losing £600/month on Larry doing data entry, plus another
£1,000/month in churn from rushed onboarding. You've been aware of it for
a year, you tried ChatGPT yourself but couldn't get it production-ready,
and ideally you'd want something running in 6 weeks with weekly check-ins.
Did I get that right?"
```

This restatement does three jobs:
1. Proves you listened.
2. Locks the prospect to their stated facts.
3. Pre-empts later "well actually I didn't quite say that" wiggle.

### 10.2 The 3-pillar structure

Three pillars, each tied to something the prospect said:

**Pillar 1 — The audit (or the offer, if product sale)**
- For audit route: explain why scoping is paid and structured.
- Use the contractor / extension analogy: *"You don't get a builder who shows up and starts knocking down walls without measuring first."*

**Pillar 2 — Stated buying signal #1**
- E.g. weekly check-ins → "Friday written updates standard."

**Pillar 3 — Stated buying signal #2**
- E.g. industry expertise → "That's exactly why the audit exists — without your context we wouldn't build."

### 10.3 The mini-yes after each pillar

```
"Does that feel like what you'd want?"
```

Three mini-yeses lower the perceived weight of the final yes (the price/signature ask).

### 10.4 Stating the price

State the audit fee (or product price) directly, with no apology and no hedging. Then ask for the next concrete step:
```
"The audit is [€X]. I'd send the agreement today, and we kick off [date].
Want me to send it through?"
```

### 10.5 The audit-as-commitment principle

The audit is not a free check that lets the prospect "decide later". It is a paid commitment with skin in the game. Frame it that way: *"This is them inviting you in to fix the problem, not asking you to scope it for free."*

## 12. Cheat sheet

### 12.1 Timing

| Stage | Time window | Anchor question |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Frame | 0–3 min | *"Sound good?"* |
| 2. Discovery | 3–18 min | *"What specifically? When did this first come up in a meeting?"* |
| 3. Pre-handle | 18–22 min | *"What's gotten in the way?"* |
| 4. Alignment | 22–26 min | *"How would you want this to work?"* |
| 5. Vision | 26–32 min | *"What does fixing this unlock?"* |
| 6. Last check | 32–34 min | *"Anything else I should know?"* |
| 7. Pitch | 34–43 min | Restate + 3 pillars + price |
| 8. Close | 43–45 min | (separate skill) |

### 12.2 Five questions to memorize

1. *"What made you actually jump on this call?"* — re-anchor to yes
2. *"When did this first come up in a meeting?"* — qualifier
3. *"What's gotten in the way of fixing this?"* — pre-handler
4. *"How would you want this to work?"* — buying signals
5. *"Anything else I should know before I get into my bit?"* — last sweep

## 13. Worked scenarios

### 13.1 Scenario A — The lead-hungry service business

**Prospect:** Owner of a 12-person home renovation company. Loses inquiries that come in outside business hours.

**Discovery flow:**
- *"What made you book?"* → *"Saw your post about AI for trades."*
- *"What specifically?"* → *"We're missing leads from our website at night."*
- *"When did this first come up in a meeting?"* → *"About 8 months ago."*
- *"How many inquiries a week?"* → *"Maybe 30."*
- *"What % do you reckon you lose because no one replies fast enough?"* → *"Probably 40%."*
- *"Average job value?"* → *"£8,000."*
- **Quantified gap:** 12 lost leads/week × £8k × ~10% close rate = ~£10k/week or ~£500k/year in lost revenue.

**Pre-handling:** *"What's stopped you fixing this?"* → *"We tried hiring a night-shift admin, didn't work out."* → *"What about that didn't work?"* → *"Couldn't justify the cost for the volume."*

**Alignment:** *"If we fixed this — how would you want it to work?"* → *"Instant reply, qualifies the lead, books a callback for the morning. Integrates with our CRM."*

**Vision:** *"What does an extra 5 jobs/month do for the business?"* → *"We'd finally hire that second project manager I've been putting off."*

**Point of no return:** *"Anything else?"* → *"Just need to know if it integrates with our CRM."*

**Pitch (audit route):**
1. Audit — 2-week scoping including CRM integration analysis
2. Build a 24/7 AI responder that qualifies + books (matches buying signal #1)
3. Weekly performance reports (matches buying signal #2 about visibility)

→ State audit price. Close.

### 13.2 Scenario B — The "just tell me the price" prospect

**Prospect (3 min in):** *"Look, can you just tell me roughly what this costs?"*

**You:**
```
"Totally fair. Quick analogy though — when your car breaks, the garage
doesn't quote you in the parking lot. They run diagnostics first because
the price depends on what's wrong. Mind if I ask 3-4 questions first so I
can actually give you a real number? Otherwise I'd just be guessing and
you'd hate me later."
```

→ If they relax: continue with discovery normally.
→ If they push back again:
```
"I hear you. Honestly, if budget's the only thing holding you up, that
tells me this might not be the right fit yet. Want me to send some
resources instead?"
```

This is a polite no-go. The prospect was window-shopping; you've reclaimed your time.

### 13.3 Scenario C — The "tried another agency" prospect

**Prospect:** *"Yeah we worked with [other agency] last year, didn't go well."*

**You:** *"Sorry to hear. Help me understand — what about the experience didn't meet what you were hoping for?"*

**Prospect:** *"They built something but it broke after 2 months and we couldn't get hold of them."*

**Internal note:** They're buying ongoing support and reliability, not just a build.

**You:** *"Got it. So if we worked together, I'm guessing reliability and access matter more than just shipping the thing?"*
**Prospect:** *"Exactly."*

**Pitch pillar #2** becomes: *"You mentioned ongoing support and access were a big issue last time — every client of ours has a Slack channel with direct access and a monthly health check."*

You've sold them on the exact thing the previous agency failed at. That is why surfacing the previous-agency story early is valuable.

### 13.4 Scenario D — The no-go

**Prospect:** Solo founder, no revenue yet, asking about *"AI to find me customers."* No quantifiable problem, no budget, no urgency. Has never had a sales meeting because there is no team.

**You:**
```
"Appreciate you being open. Honestly, based on what you've shared, I don't
think we'd be the right fit yet — most of our value comes from automating
things you're already doing manually with people, and it sounds like you're
still figuring out the manual version. I'd hate to take your money and not
move the needle. Worth circling back once you've got 5–10 paying customers
and you know your sales process."
```

The prospect respects you more for this than for a forced sale. Long-term referral upside.

## 14. Anti-patterns

| Anti-pattern | Why it fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Skipping the frame | Loss of authority; prospect runs the call | Always set the doctor frame and get a "sound good?" |
| Asking only one layer of questions about the problem | Problem stays vague; no number; no urgency | Layer 3–4 deep, always end with a £/$ figure |
| Pitching before pre-handling | Objection lands at minute 45; deal dies | Always run the *"what's gotten in the way?"* question |
| Saying "we do that" during alignment | You leak your hand; the prospect stops volunteering | Just collect. Pitch later. |
| Avoiding the no-go | Onboards bad-fit clients who leak P&L and reputation | Run the process, then state the no clearly |
| Treating "we tried [agency]" as a negative | You miss the gold (their stated criteria) | Always probe what didn't meet expectations |
| Quoting on the discovery call when the offer is custom | Either underprice and lose margin, or overprice to cover risk | Sell the audit. Quote after diagnosis. |
| Blaming the prospect when they don't close | You lose the lesson | Did you actually run all 7 stages? Most "bad prospects" were poorly run calls. |

## 15. Linguistic toolbox

### 15.1 The analogies

| Situation | Analogy |
|---|---|
| Why you ask questions before quoting | Doctor / car-garage / contractor measuring before quoting an extension |
| Why the prospect should let you lead | Caddy to Tiger Woods |
| Why the audit isn't free | Builder doesn't knock walls without measurement |
| Why the prospect can't expect a price up front | "Would you trust a doctor who handed you a prescription as you walked in?" |

### 15.2 Soft-phrasing patterns

| Hard version | Softer reframe |
|---|---|
| *"Why didn't you fix this?"* | *"What's gotten in the way of fixing this?"* |
| *"What was wrong with that agency?"* | *"Help me understand — what about that experience didn't meet your expectations?"* |
| *"When can you start?"* | *"How would you want this to work in terms of timeline?"* |
| *"Don't you have budget?"* | *"If budget is something we should account for, what range are you thinking?"* |
| *"Just tell me the problem"* | *"What specifically do you need help with?"* |

### 15.3 The four reframes

1. **Common-sense reframe:** Use a third-party scenario the prospect already accepts (doctor, garage, builder).
2. **Behavioral reframe:** When they cite reasons for past inaction, ask *"What's different now?"*
3. **Investment reframe:** "Spending" → "investment". Companies start because they invest to get something back.
4. **Self-attempt reframe:** "Out of curiosity — what stopped you from doing this yourself?" exposes the failed DIY without confrontation.
